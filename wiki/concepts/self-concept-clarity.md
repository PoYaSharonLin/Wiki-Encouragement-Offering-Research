# Self-Concept Clarity (SCC)

## Definition

Proposed by Campbell et al. (1996):

> SCC is the extent to which the contents of an individual's self-concept are clearly and confidently defined, internally consistent, and temporally stable.

SCC encompasses three dimensions:
- **Clarity**: Having a clear, confident definition of oneself
- **Consistency**: Self-beliefs do not contradict each other
- **Temporal Stability**: Views of oneself remain stable across time

## Distinction from Self-Consistency (Decision made 2026-03-24)

| Dimension | SCC (Campbell, 1996) | Self-Consistency (Gergen & Morse, 1967) |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| Scope | Three dimensions (clarity + consistency + temporal stability) | Narrower, primarily focused on internal consistency |
| Scale availability | Widely used with a complete standard scale | Less cited; scale harder to obtain |
| Theoretical status | Broadly accepted measure | Self-Consistency is a precursor concept to SCC |

**Rationale for choosing SCC**:
> Given that the study is already theoretically ambitious (combining promotion focus with coping appraisal), using a more widely accepted scale helps maintain research rigor.

## SCC Scale (9 items, Campbell et al., 1996)

1. My beliefs about myself often conflict with one another
2. I seldom experience conflict between the different aspects of my personality
3. On one day I might have one opinion of myself and on another day I might have a different opinion
4. My beliefs about myself seem to change very frequently
5. If I were asked to describe my personality, my description might end up being different from one day to another day
6. It is often hard for me to make up my mind about things because I don't really know what I want
7. I spend a lot of time wondering about what kind of person I really am
8. When I think about the kind of person I have been in the past, I'm not sure what I was really like
9. Sometimes I think I know other people better than I know myself

## Role in the Research

Serves as a **control variable** to capture the potential confounding effect that participants with a less clear self-concept may be more susceptible to message framing.
