# Protection Motivation Theory (PMT)

## Definition

Proposed by Rogers (1975, 1983), explains individuals' behavioral motivation when facing health threats:

```
Protection Motivation = [(Intrinsic + Extrinsic Rewards) - (Severity × Vulnerability)]
                      + [(Response Efficacy × Self-Efficacy) - Response Costs]
```

## Core Components

**Threat Appraisal**
- Severity: How serious is the threat?
- Vulnerability: How likely am I to be affected?
→ Comparative baseline for this research: Fear Appeal is the operationalization of Threat Appraisal

**Coping Appraisal**
- Response Efficacy: "Does doing this actually work?"
- Self-Efficacy: "Am I capable of doing it?"
- Response Cost: "How difficult/costly is it to do this?"

## PMT Evolution Timeline

| Year | Author | Contribution |
|------|--------|--------------|
| 1975 | Rogers | Proposed the original PMT with Fear Appeal at its core |
| 1977 | Bandura | Formally defined self-efficacy |
| 1983 | Rogers (revised) | Formally integrated response efficacy and self-efficacy |
| 1983 | Maddux & Rogers | Established self-efficacy as the fourth element of PMT |
| 1988 | Robberson & Rogers | Proposed Positive Appeal as an alternative to Negative Appeal |

## Relevance to This Research

This study treats PMT as "an existing paradigm to be challenged":
1. Fear Appeal (PMT-based) may trigger negative emotions in certain health contexts and backfire
2. Ruby (2023) used PMT + RAM slider as the control condition
3. This study extends Keller (2006)'s work, replacing Threat Appraisal with Promotion-Efficacy Fit
4. Retains PMT's Coping Appraisal components (response efficacy + self-efficacy) but reorients the motivational direction
