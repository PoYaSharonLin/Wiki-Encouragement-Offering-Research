# Self-Construal

## Definition

The way individuals construct a sense of "self," originally proposed as a dichotomy by Markus & Kitayama (1991):

- **Independent Self**: The self is primarily defined by personal traits, goals, and attitudes; culturally associated with Western individualism
- **Interdependent Self**: The self is primarily defined by relationships, roles, and social context; culturally associated with Asian and African collectivism

## Evolution of Scale Selection

### Singelis (1994) — Two-Dimensional Scale
- Measures both Independent and Interdependent dimensions
- Scale items available on their laboratory website

### Vignoles et al. (2016) — Seven-Dimensional Scale (adopted by this study)
Moves beyond the East-West binary and proposes seven dimensions:
**self-reliance, self-containment, difference, self-interest, consistency, self-direction, self-expression**

**Reason for adoption** (decision made 2026-03-17):
> A preference for the Vignoles et al. (2016) framework, because individuals lower in "consistency" or "self-direction" are more susceptible to persuasion — which aligns more closely with the intervention mechanism this study examines.

## Role in the Research

Serves as a **control variable** to rule out the confounding effect that participants with interdependent self-construal may be more responsive to promotion-focused messages.

Theoretical rationale:
- Interdependent self-construers may be more sensitive to social norms and thus more responsive to positive interventions
- Independent self-construers may report honestly by default due to personal excellence motivation
- Therefore, self-construal must be controlled to isolate the intervention effect

## Key Dimensions (Most Relevant to This Research)

1. **Self-direction**: Whether an individual has an independent will to act
2. **Consistency**: Whether an individual has a stable self-concept across time

Illustration logic (Slide 41):
> "I may be susceptible to the intervention because I am not a person with strong self-direction / strong consistency"
